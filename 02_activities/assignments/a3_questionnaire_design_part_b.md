An observational study could analyze existing data sources, such as music streaming service trends (e.g., Spotify, Apple Music), historical charts, or social media discussions, to examine how different age groups engage with music over time. Researchers could track how listening habits shift with age by studying user playlists, concert attendance, or online music discussions. Additionally, a longitudinal study could follow a group of individuals over several years to observe natural changes in their music preferences.  

However, an observational study would struggle to capture perceptions of music taste changes, nostalgia, and personal reflections on past and present preferences. The survey format allows direct insight into why people feel their taste has changed, which cannot be easily observed through external data. Self-reported reasons for music preference shifts, emotional connections to past music, and subjective opinions on contemporary popular music require direct questioning.  

In summary, while an observational study could provide quantitative insights into listening patterns across different ages, a survey is better suited for understanding the personal and subjective aspects of music taste evolution.
